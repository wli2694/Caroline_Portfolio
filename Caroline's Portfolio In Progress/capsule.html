    Capsule

    Online platform for Carnegie Mellon University students to reflect on their values and goals

    Scope: Class Project in Service Design with Jodi Forlizzi

    Client: The Simon Initiative at CMU

    Team: Eunsol Byun, Catherine Chiodo, Gena Hong, Jasmine Kim, Caroline Win

    My Role: Service Designer, User Researcher

    Timeline: Sept - Dec 2016

    Problem Statement

    The Simon Initiative at Carnegie Mellon University presented us with a problem statement: “Undergraduate students at Carnegie Mellon possess great technical skills but struggle overall with soft skills such as communication and leadership.”

    How might we design a service that enhances writing, reflection, and leadership skills in students at CMU?

    Research

    As a team we sought to understand the current problem space from multiple stakeholders’ perspectives and discover the strategies and trends at other universities.

    We found that while almost all universities, including CMU, utilized curriculum requirements and communication centers, many also had an "x-factor" initiative that promoted student communication skills in unique ways. For example—student conferences to share personal projects and work, a 1-month no-class period for exploring interests, and cultural and humanities initiatives.

    We found that CMU was generally lacking in this "x-factor."

    Synthesis & Analysis

    Initial Interview Findings

    Faculty understand communication is important but prioritize other skills and requirements first. Generally, students have little enthusiasm for the courses actually focused on writing and communication.

    Students therefore gain communication skills primarily via extracurricular activities and student-to-student interaction, not schoolwork.

    Directed Storytelling Findings

    Being primarily focused on graduation requirements, advising at CMU does little to help students with understanding what soft skills are important and how to develop these skills to achieve personal goals.

    Concept Map

    Many students at CMU are discovering opportunities to develop leadership and communication skills, but it's not happening in a systematic or consistent way.

    What if advising at CMU focused on students’ goals and the big picture instead of simply graduating, the lowest standard?

    Formal Advising Service Model

    Students are overwhelmed by the multitude of advisors they need to coordinate with over short time periods—15 minutes per session.

    Overall, the formal advising process for CMU undergraduates is short, fragmented, and highly dependent on student initative.

    Informal Advising Service Model

    Because of the lack of formal guidance, students rely mainly on their informal advising network of peers to navigate their decisions and experiences in college.

    By encouraging students to consider their values and long-term goals, students will be able to better identify the skills they need to succeed and likely feel more motivated to develop their soft skills.

    Ideation

    Based on the opportunity area identified, we generated 18 ideas to encourage communication skills in the realm of formal and informal advising.

    Personas

    We created 5 personas to facilitate ideation and keep the original goals in mind.

    Opportunities & Concepts

    We then clustered the ideas by theme and created a report describing the 9 best concepts.

    Storyboarding & Concept Evaluation

    After professor and class feedback, we narrowed our ideas down to 6 concepts which we sketched out as storyboards.

    We tested our concepts via speed-dating with different stakeholders.

    4faculty advisors

    5undergraduate students

    1alumni

    6 Final Concepts

    • Course & Activity Explorer: tags based on topics and skills covered
    • Advisor Scheduling & Notetaking System: helps advisors organize and recall student info
    • Alumni Data Resource: provides info on courses taken and current jobs of alumni
    • Roadmap of Resources: provides just-in-time resources as students need them
    • Interclass Advising Groups: students assigned to "teams" that act as support network
    • Student Reflections: students answer questions about goals and values before advising sessions

    Key Findings from Concept Evaluation

    • Students want to have one centralized source for resources and advising.
    • Students and advisors do not want a system that sets a rigid path or prevents students from exploring.
    • An advisor scheduling system and alumni data resource may already be in the works.
    • Advisors have busy schedules and cannot easily take on large amounts of work.
    • Advisors want to optimize their time to really help students.
    • Reflections could be very helpful in facilitating more productive conversations with advisors.

    Selecting Our Final Concept

    A culture of reflection will allow students to better decide their own priorities, values, life goals, and desired experiences. We believe that this will support a CMU where being a leader, sharing personal stories with others, and articulating what matters most is part of the common social fabric, shared by students, faculty, staff, and other stakeholders.

    Final Proposal: Capsule

    Capsule is an online platform for students to reflect, explore, and share personal insights throughout their college experience.

    Components

    1. Reflection Journal: students complete interactive and thought-provoking activities such as selecting their core values or creating different 5-year plans
    2. Communication Platform: students share their reflections with their advisors, family, and friends and obtain feedback if desired
    3. Interactive Art Installation: data installation on campus allows the entire CMU community to experience student reflections as they happen on Capsule

    Service Blueprint

    Final Presentation & Video

    For our final presentation, we created an animated stop-motion video that shares a narrative about the interaction a student would have with Capsule.

    Next Steps

    To improve our design, we would further develop details on the touchpoints of Capsule and how they would be operationalized. For example, the reflection journal activities could be created by individual advisors or the Deans of individual departments. Students may access Capsule through another frequently used CMU website, such as SIO (course registration system).

    Overall, this project allowed me to experience the complexity of service design. There are so many factors and people that need to be considered. With pros and cons to every idea, it is extremely difficult to ideate and decide on one concept that is most promising.
