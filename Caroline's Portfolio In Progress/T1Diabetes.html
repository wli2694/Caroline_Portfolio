    Type 1 Diabetes Education

    Co-participatory design to improve lives of Type 1 diabetes patients

    Scope: Class Project in Design Studio with Elizabeth Sanders, PhD

    Team: Abel Hernandez, Darwin Muljono, Caroline Win, Tally Wolff

    My Role: Service Designer, Design Researcher, Facilitator

    Timeline: Jan - May 2016

    Project Brief

    Type 1 Diabetes (T1D) is a chronic illness that accounts for between 5% and 10% of all diabetes cases. It is often first diagnosed in childhood, and about 80,000 children develop the disease each year.

    How can we utilize co-design to create concepts that will improve the lives of those living with T1D?

    Selecting a Focus

    As a class, we mapped out the primary stakeholders involved with Type 1 Diabetes, grouping them into the following:

    • patient & immediate circle of care (friends, family)
    • school & work
    • government & insurance companies
    • health care workers
    • medical device companies & the food industry

    Our team decided to focus on the school & work category, with the goal of designing a solution that can help schools and workplaces better support students and employees with Type 1 Diabetes.

    Understanding the Patient Experience

    Workshop with College Diabetes Network

    A few team members attended a CDN meeting and conducted a mini workshop. Each CDN member was asked to come up with 3 or 4 ideas, themes, or challenges relating to T1D that they hoped we would be able to explore on this project. Below is a summary of key findings.

    • Confusion between Type 1 and Type 2 diabetes — often leads to public misconceptions and other psychological impacts
    • Lack of education in the general public about T1D and how to support people with T1D
    • Training process for T1D medical devices is not fun, engaging, or memorable
    • Transition from pediatrics to adult care often lacks support or guidance
    • Circles of care for T1D patients are disconnected
    • Mental health issues more common amongst people living with T1D

    Type 1 diabetes never stops. 24/7 management is stressful and exhausting for patients.

    Patient Interviews

    We also recruited 4 students from the CDN to interview to better understand their experiences and challenges. To facilitate the interview, we provided a paper timeline, cut-out icons, images, markers, and tape to allow participants to map out key positive and negative experiences in their life related to their diabetes.

    Key Interview Findings

    • Biggest limitation in school is sports — troublesome to stop and check blood sugar
    • Teens feel ashamed when they don’t manage their diabetes perfectly
    • Diabetes camp is one very positive experience that allows kids with diabetes to feel more comfortable
    • Overall, there is a lack of support and understanding for Type 1 diabetes patients
    • Confusion between Type 1 and Type 2 leads to people judging and blaming T1D patients for getting their disease

    Immersion Experience

    To better understand the experience of living with T1D, our team spent a week using various devices that patients with diabetes use. I spent a week wearing a continuous glucose monitor (CGM) on my abdomen. Initially, I felt overwhelmed with learning how to use the device and remembering all of the steps. Insertion of the monitor was painful.

    During the week, it was interesting to be able to see how my blood glucose changed with various foods. The site became itchy, after a few days, and the adhesive started coming off. Most frustrating was the finger sticks. One was fine, but having to do multiple in a day was not fun, and I would get bruises on my finger. It also takes a lot of time and effort to track things like carbs and exercise. Overall, I can imagine that it must be very overwhelming especially when patients are first diagnosed as children and have to adjust to all of these changes.

    Co-Design Workshop

    Planning & Preparations

    We decided that we would invite education and CDN students to come together and brainstorm their own ideas for improving the experience of elementary and middle school students with T1D and ways to impart education to teachers, nurses, and community members.

    We would provide all participants with a copy of Insuman, a fun comic that explains the experience of getting diagnosed with T1D in school.

    Activity 1. Design a School

    Each team will first be presented with a diagram of a school building and blocks representing various touchpoints (classroom, clinic, gym, etc.). They will use markers, paper, blocks, and other materials to design improvements at these touchpoints.

    Activity 2. Implementation of Design

    On a blank diagram of the socio-ecological circle, each team would then use markers and post-its to show how their design solutions impact each circle and what steps would need to be taken to make their solutions a reality.

    Pilot Workshop

    We tested our workshop plan with a few classmates, determining that we needed to have clearer and simpler instructions.

    Workshop Day

    8 participants came out for our workshop, and we split them into two separate groups. Overall, it was quite a success! Participants were engaged and gave a lot of great insights. It was most challenging to make sure the activities were completed in our given timeframe.

    Key Ideas from Participants

    • Increase awarenesss of T1D to reduce stigma, bullying, and mixing it up with T2D
    • Diabetes education should be incorporated into the curriculum (math, science, etc.)
    • Teachers should learn more about diabetes (e.g. through training) so they feel comfortable with having T1D children in their care
    • Teachers that find success should share their ideas with other teachers
    • There should be a dedicated RN for each school—often not the case
    • Build strong communication between teacher, parent, student, and peers
    • T1D child should be at the center of care
