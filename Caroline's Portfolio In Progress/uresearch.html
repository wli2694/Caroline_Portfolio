    uResearch

    Search and collaboration tool for academic researchers

    Scope: Class Project in User-Centered Research & Evaluation with Amy Ogan, Jim Morris, and Chris Connors

    Client: uReveal

    Team: Tony Deng, Zhi Jia Guo, Shannon Sullivan, Caroline Win, Tianxin Xu

    My Role: User Researcher

    Timeline: Sept - Nov 2016

    Project Brief

    uReveal’s data-mining software allows a user to index and search multiple sources including private databases and public newsfeeds and create specialized dictionaries of synonyms. As the software is sold mostly to investigative agencies like NCIS, uReveal asked us to explore additional markets and applications of their technology. Our team focused on academic researchers as our primary user group.

    How might we apply data-mining technology to improve the effectiveness and efficiency of academic researchers in their daily work?

    Contextual Inquiry

    Our first step was to conduct interviews to better understand the values, goals, and pain points of our primary user group: academic researchers.

    Interviews with Academic Researchers

    We created interview protocols and met with each participant for 30 minutes, asking questions such as “Tell me about the last time you searched for information on your research topic,” and “Can you think of a situation when you were unable to find information you were looking for?” We also observed the participants as they worked.

    Participants Interviewed

    1business & economics librarian

    1HCI professor

    1post-doctoral fellow in HCI


    All interviewees signed consent forms.

    Interpretation Session

    We then met for 6 hours as a team and utilized Karen Holtzblatt’s techniques to recount the interviews, note down key findings, and create sequence-flow models for each interviewee.

    Primary Pain Points for Academics

    • Difficult to be aware of hundreds of databases and resources, which are variable in quality and usability
    • Specific papers are difficult to find because Google does not sort by field
    • Important information can be missed if proper search terms are not used
    • News aggregators do not usually provide enough information
    • Some databases do not allow the user to download information
    • Storing and organizing all sources is a tedious process

    Synthesis & Analysis

    Affinity Diagramming

    Taking our 150+ notes, we began to group them into affinity clusters. We then conducted a"walk-the-wall" activity where each team member added questions, insights, or design ideas to the affinity wall.

    Collaborative & High Quality

    • Collaboration plays an important role throughout my research process.
    • I need tools that enable me to easily share info, collect feedback, and work with others.
    • I perform time-consuming analyses of text by hand because I do not trust technical solutions.
    • I want to ensure my research introduces new ideas and adds value.
    • It is important for me to keep up with my field. I do this via communicating with colleagues, reading papers, and skimming sites.

    Efficient

    • I want a more efficient way of organizing my work and resources.
    • I don’t have enough time to keep up with the frontier and also maintain a good work-life balance.
    • I want to easily capture key points from a large quantity of literature.

    Powerful, Intuitive & Customizable

    • Students are learning to do research and want tools that are easy to access and understand.
    • I don’t believe current search tools are powerful or customizable enough.
    • I want credible databases with a large quantity of data.

    Consolidated Models

    We then combined our individual sequence-flow models into a consolidated sequence-flow model, including all primary stakeholders and tools in the process. We also envisioned a future sequence-flow model, depicting a more ideal scenario based on our insights and design ideas.

    We also created a cultural model to represent the different forces influencing each stakeholder and entity.

    Academics have trouble finding credible and specific content they need in a time-efficient manner while seeking information to inspire or inform their research.

    Designing & Evaluating Ideas

    Visioning

    Based on our research, we conducted a visioning session to create 6 different concepts.

    • Quickly sharing interesting research with colleagues via chat system
    • Web browser plug-in for filtering and recommendations
    • Mobile app for researching, sharing, and saving interesting research on the go
    • Web app where researchers can get crowd-sourced summaries of papers
    • Weekly newsletters that provide curated information relevant to user's topics of interest
    • System that learns a researcher's paper browsing habits and recommends more papers based on them

    Storyboards & Speed-Dating

    We identified pros and cons of each concept, refined and updated them, and created storyboards for 3 final concepts. We then used these storyboards to conduct speed-dating with 3 PhD students and 1 associate professor.

    Key Findings from Speed-Dating

    • For crowd-sourced paper summaries, interviewees liked that it would save them time
    • However, they felt that the summaries would need to be very specific to be useful—responding to specific research questions, for example
    • General paper summaries can already be found in evidence-based reviews and peer reviews
    • Strong interest in the idea of having weekly summaries of news and latest papers
    • Some tools already address identified needs, but perhaps not reliably enough for researchers to ubiquitously adapt
    • Privacy is a concern in sharing research information with colleagues

    Final Proposal: uResearch

    uResearch is an all-in-one research tool that provides academics with connections to colleagues and content they need at the time they need it.

    • Centralized searching, organizing, and sharing of papers
    • Browse and save links from a variety of academic resources such as JSTOR, ACM, and more.
    • Search for papers based on field, conference, author, keyword
    • Add tags to papers
    • Customized weekly newsletters of latest news, papers, and research findings in topic of interest
    • Private forums to initiate discussions and potentially forge collaboration and co-authorships

    Why will it address the need?

    A central location that can both retrieve and store information will reduce the number of sites that academics need to visit when conducting research, making their workflow more efficient.

    Because researchers already use many online resources to conduct their research (e.g. Google Scholar) and to store and categorize papers (e.g. Mendeley), having an online tool that combines these two processes should be easy for researchers to adopt.
